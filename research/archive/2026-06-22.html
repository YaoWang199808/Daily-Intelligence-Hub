Engineering Structures

Experimental investigation of ultimate lateral resistance of superstructure–steel pipe pile foundation–soil systems considering local buckling–induced strength deterioration of pile members

Authors: Mutsuki Sato, Yoshihiro Kimura

Institution: Tohoku University

Published: 2026-06-20

Journal: Engineering Structures

Method: Hybrid

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Steel pipe piles supporting buildings may lose lateral resistance during strong earthquakes because local buckling at pile heads degrades member strength under high axial force. Although the lateral resistance of single piles and pile groups has been widely studied, a rational framework for evaluating the ultimate lateral resistance of superstructure–pile foundation–soil systems while accounting for local buckling–induced strength deterioration is still lacking. This study proposes such a framework by integrating experimentally identified collapse mechanisms, finite element evaluation of degraded pile bending resistance, and virtual-work-based system analysis. Centrifuge lateral loading tests on two-pile foundations embedded in sand revealed that the ultimate state is governed by a two-hinge mechanism in each pile and that system collapse is triggered by hinge formation in the embedded portion of the back pile after progressive axial force redistribution and pile-head local buckling. Based on these observations, finite element analyses were used to quantify the degraded bending moment after local buckling through a plasticity-ratio-based procedure, and the system ultimate lateral resistance was then evaluated by accounting for the internal work of both piles and soil, including group pile effects. The study demonstrates that local buckling–induced deterioration is a decisive factor in the maximum lateral resistance of steel pipe pile foundation systems and provides a mechanics-based basis for performance-based design of building pile foundations.

Comparison between homogeneous beech and hybrid beech-silver fir glulam beams made with short supply chain timber

Authors: Martina Sciomenta, Alfredo Peditto, Michela Nocetti, Michele Brunetti, Manuela Romagnoli, Massimo Fragiacomo, Giuseppe Scarascia Mugnozza

Institution: University of L'Aquila, Institute of Biosciences and Bioresources, Università degli Studi della Tuscia

Published: 2026-06-19

Journal: Engineering Structures

Method: Other

Citations: 0

Abstract:

The growing request of timber for high performance constructions coupled with the depletion of conifer resources due to the ecosystem modification caused by global warming, makes it necessary to explore alternative supply sources. Additionally, the increased emphasis on sustainability within the supply chain has strongly influenced the worldwide relaunch of small local businesses, aiming to provide regionally sourced materials while reducing environmental impact and fostering economic resilience. This study investigates the mechanical properties and bonding quality of homogeneous beech and hybrid beech-silver fir glulam beams produced using short supply chain timber from Southern Italy. This research aims at demonstrating the feasibility of a local production using underused hardwood species as beech. Mechanical characterization included bending and shear tests, as well as evaluations of finger joint and glue line performance. The achieved evidence demonstrated the goodness of both configurations that meet or exceed the standards for structural timber. In particular, the hybrid configuration proved to have comparable mechanical properties for homogeneous and hybrid beams while being lighter and more material-efficient. Bonding quality assessments revealed that finger joints performed satisfactorily for both species, with values exceeding the characteristic strength of the respective timber grades. Glue line strength was comparable to other literature results, though homogeneous beech beams faced challenges in delamination resistance, highlighting the need for further adhesive optimization. Conversely, hybrid beams exhibited superior bonding durability, effectively addressing hardwood gluing challenges. This research proved the potential of hardwood use for the realization of engineered products for structural applications; it also highlights the potential of hybrid configurations in optimizing material performance to realize high-performance structural components while supporting local timber supply chain. Future research should refine bonding techniques for hardwood glulam and explore alternative adhesives to further improve durability and performance.
