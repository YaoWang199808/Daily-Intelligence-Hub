Balancing accuracy, interpretability, and cost in groundwater quality analysis and prediction using advanced hybrid models

Authors: Bilel Zeroualı, Abdessamed Derdour, Amnah Aldohan, Zaki Abda, Nadjm Bailek, Celso Augusto Guimarães Santos, Boualem Remini, Youssef M. Youssef

Institution: Hassiba Benbouali University of Chlef, Princess Nourah bint Abdulrahman University, University of Guelma, Ahmed Draia University, University of Blida, Suez University

Published: 2026-07-01

Journal: Measurement

Method: Hybrid

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Groundwater quality degradation driven by climate change and anthropogenic pressures represents a major challenge for sustainable irrigation in arid and semiarid regions. Although artificial intelligence (AI) methods have been widely applied for Irrigation Water Quality Index (IWQI) prediction, many existing models lack interpretability and do not consider monitoring costs. Therefore, this study develops an integrated framework that combines predictive performance, explainability, and cost-effectiveness for groundwater quality assessment. A dataset of 621 groundwater samples from Béchar, Algeria, including 10 physicochemical parameters, was analyzed using two modeling approaches: (i) a stacked ensemble integrating Isolation Forest, Random Committee, Random Forest, and CatBoost with logistic regression, and (ii) a CNN encoder–decoder model. SHAP analysis was used to interpret model predictions, while feature-based optimization was applied to evaluate cost-effectiveness. The results identified electrical conductivity (EC), sodium (Na + ), and chloride (Cl − ) as the dominant IWQI drivers, with a strong negative correlation between IWQI and Na + (r ≈ − 0.94). The CNN encoder–decoder achieved the best overall performance, with Model M4 producing micro-F1 and macro-F1 scores of 0.957 and 0.947, respectively. SHAP analysis confirmed Na + and EC as the most influential variables. Cost analysis showed that Model M9, based on only four salinity-related parameters and a total cost of USD 39, maintained an accuracy above 92% and achieved the highest cost-effectiveness score of 0.02358. These findings demonstrate that interpretable and cost-optimized deep-learning models can support reliable groundwater quality assessment and provide practical decision-support tools for sustainable irrigation water management in data-limited regions.

Linking Coupled Reservoir Modeling to Dynamic Rupture Modeling: An Automated Workflow to Simulate Induced Seismicity in Faulted Geothermal Systems

Authors: Rahim Habibi, T Ulrich, Alice-Agnes Gabriel, Thomas Köhl, Emmanuel Gaucher

Institution: Karlsruhe Institute of Technology, Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität München, Scripps Institution of Oceanography, University of California San Diego

Published: 2026-07-21

Journal: Rock Mechanics and Rock Engineering

Method: Numerical Simulation

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Abstract Induced seismicity associated with subsurface fluid injection poses significant challenges for geothermal reservoir management. While quasi-static models capture long-term pressure, temperature, and stress evolution, dynamic rupture codes resolve transient earthquake mechanics. In this study, we develop a fully coupled framework that integrates both approaches to simulate fault reactivation and induced seismicity in a synthetic geothermal system. Unlike previous one-way coupled frameworks, our approach implements a closed-loop feedback, where stress redistribution from dynamically simulated seismic events is fed back into the quasi-static THM reservoir model. A 3D thermo-hydro-mechanical model tracks stress build-up during injection until a failure criterion is met, triggering a transition to a dynamic rupture solver that computes spontaneous 3D rupture propagation and seismic wave radiation. An automated coupling protocol ensures seamless data exchange between solvers. We demonstrate the framework by simulating two injection-induced events on a single-fault reservoir model. Results show that event-driven fault displacements and dynamic stress transfers significantly alter reservoir stress states. Simulated Coulomb stress changes, focal mechanisms, and slip distributions align with the ambient stress field and fault geometry. This closed-loop approach provides a robust tool for assessing seismic risk and optimizing injection strategies in geothermal environments.
