Accessible and reproducible measurement of carbonation depth in cementitious materials using an open-source image analysis framework

Authors: Edgar Perin Moraes, Rafael Augusto Ventura, Glauco Soares Braga, Ana Cecília Vieira da Nóbrega, Bruno Costa, Rodrigo César Santiago, Fabiano Galbiati, Júlio Cézar de Oliveira Freitas

Institution: Universidade Federal do Rio Grande do Norte, Federal Center for Technological Education of Minas Gerais, Universidade Federal do Estado do Rio de Janeiro

Published: 2026-06-14

Journal: Measurement

Method: Experimental

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Cement-based materials are among the most widely used materials worldwide, and carbonation is one of the main mechanisms affecting their long-term durability. Carbonation depth is a key measurand for assessing the durability of cementitious materials. However, its determination typically relies on manual interpretation of phenolphthalein treated fracture surfaces, leading to operator-dependent variability and limited reproducibility. This study presents Carbon Depth, an image-based measurement framework for automated quantification of carbonation depth from digital images. The workflow integrates image acquisition, preprocessing, color-based segmentation, carbonation front detection, and automated extraction of depth metrics. The method was applied to three cementitious systems (latex-modified, silica-modified, and reference Class G) exposed to accelerated carbonation for 30, 60, and 90 days after initial curing (CO 2 /H 2 S, 3000 psi, 60 °C). A total of 54 fractured surfaces were analyzed and compared with manual measurements obtained by two independent operators. The results showed a strong agreement, with a mean bias of 0.10 mm, RMSE of 0.22 mm, and R 2 of 0.973. Bland–Altman analysis indicated consistent performance across the measurement range. A semi-synthetic dataset (14 images) was further used to assess robustness under varying optical conditions, yielding R 2 = 0.9991 and RMSE = 0.22 mm. The proposed approach preserves experimental trends and provides a reproducible, low-cost measurement solution with reduced operator dependency. The full processing pipeline is made available as an open-source implementation, enabling transparency, reproducibility, and adaptation across different laboratory and field conditions

A Well Log-Driven Approach for Mechanofacies Characterization via Unsupervised Learning

Authors: Josiane Branco Plantz, Ricardo Nunes

Institution: Universidade Federal de Uberlândia, Universidade Ceuma

Published: 2026-06-16

Journal: Rock Mechanics and Rock Engineering

Method: Machine Learning

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Abstract Recognizing the importance of well logging, especially sonic velocity (DT), and density (RHOB) logs, for estimating rock mechanical properties, this study evaluates the applicability of unsupervised machine learning to classify mechanofacies. To achieve this goal, a multivariate cluster-analysis workflow is proposed, using only geomechanical parameters derived from well logs as input data. This approach seeks to reduce the subjectivity associated with the mechanofacies concept and establish an easily applicable methodology capable of providing fast and consistent results, even when data availability imposes significant constraints. Accordingly, dynamic elastic properties—Young’s modulus ( E d ), shear modulus ( G d ), bulk modulus ( K d ), Poisson’s ratio ( ν )—and unconfined compressive strength (UCS) were calculated through empirical correlations, and their uncertainties were incorporated into the geomechanical model proposed. The analysis was performed on five wells in the south-central Paraná Basin, southern Brazil. The investigated successions are thick and lithologically diverse, comprising clastic and carbonate sedimentary rocks, subvolcanic igneous rocks, and, in some wells, granitic gneisses from the crystalline basement. Cluster analyses identified five clusters in each well, allowing synthesis of rock geomechanical behavior into five mechanofacies (MF1, MF2, MF3, MF4, and MF5) that show lateral continuity across the study area. MF1 and MF2, representing the least resistant rocks, occur mainly near the top of the wells, whereas MF4—with high E d , G d , K d , and UCS—is more common at depth. MF3 appears throughout multiple stratigraphic levels, indicating strong vertical heterogeneity that affects drilling and stimulation performance. MF5 is lithologically controlled and dominated by high-strength igneous rocks. Despite uncertainties and spatial limitations, the model results highlight the potential of unsupervised learning techniques for identifying mechanofacies in data-limited contexts, revealing patterns that can support future drilling and reservoir stimulation projects. A small adjustment is proposed: Mário Palmério University Center (UNIFUCAMP), Monte Carmelo, Minas Gerais, Brazil.
