Non-destructive acoustic assessment of low-cost electret microphones for data-driven monitoring of the FFF additive manufacturing process

Authors: Thiago Glissoi Lopes, Giulio Mattera, Paulo Roberto de Aguiar, Luigi Nele, Reinaldo Götz de Oliveira, Pedro Oliveira

Institution: Universidade de Sorocaba, University of Naples Federico II

Published: 2026-07-18

Journal: Measurement

Method: Experimental

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Low-cost monitoring of Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) is limited by the scarce validation of affordable acoustic sensors. Although acoustic emission sensors, piezoelectric transducers, and low-cost alternatives have been explored for additive manufacturing monitoring, the non-destructive assessment of electret microphones for FFF remains limited. This study evaluates whether inexpensive electret microphones can support FFF acoustic monitoring using a non-destructive framework based on the Pencil-Lead Break (PLB) method and a Sphere Launching Impact Test (SLIT). A piezoelectric transducer (PZT) and electret microphones were attached to the printing table of a GTMax3D A1V2 printer to benchmark their responses. Multiple PLB and SLIT tests, followed by a printing-monitoring case study, were used to assess repeatability, spectral agreement, and transducer correspondence. Results show that electret microphone responses are highly repeatable and closely match the PZT signals associated with the printing process within a narrow 60–200 Hz band. Frequency-domain analysis quantified PLB-SLIT agreement, with mean magnitude-squared coherence (MSC) values of approximately 0.81–0.92 in the 0–20 kHz band and values below 0.35 above 20 kHz, indicating strong in-band coherence and microphone-to-microphone variability. In printing monitoring, electret microphones more clearly separated external and internal pattern conditions and highlighted amplitude changes during a G-code-induced under-extrusion period in a monolayer part. Narrow-band filtering further increased agreement with the PZT, supporting focused spectral windows. Overall, the framework supports electret microphones as a viable low-cost option for scalable acoustic monitoring in additive manufacturing and highlights SLIT as a rapid non-destructive sensor-response assessment method.

Outage-resilient real-time precise point positioning with SSR prediction

Authors: Hanyang Teng, Shuqiong Liu, Xinyuan Jiang

Institution: Kunming University of Science and Technology, GFZ Helmholtz Centre for Geosciences, Technische Universität Berlin, John Deere (Germany)

Published: 2026-07-15

Journal: Measurement

Method: Experimental

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Real-time precise point positioning (PPP) based on state-space representation (SSR) corrections is widely used in high-accuracy real-time applications, but its performance degrades rapidly when SSR streams are interrupted or valid satellite corrections are temporarily unavailable. To address this problem, this study proposes an outage-resilient PPP strategy that combines three components: an issue of data (IOD) consistent transformation of SSR orbit and clock corrections to remove artificial jumps at broadcast-ephemeris switches, short-term prediction of orbit and clock SSR corrections using polynomial fitting, and adaptive down-weighting of predicted corrections in the PPP filter, followed by a gradual ramp-in when real-time SSR corrections resume. The proposed method was evaluated using GPS, Galileo, and BDS data under simulated short-term SSR outages. The overall statistical analysis was conducted using one week of data from GPS Week 2363, while representative IOD and single-station examples were taken from 20 April 2025. The results show that the proposed strategy improves positioning stability during outages and reduces discontinuities during recovery. For outages shorter than 600 s, the average RMS values in the north, east, and up components are reduced by 48.08%, 31.58%, and 51.44%, respectively, relative to the direct-recovery strategy. During the recovery period, the corresponding reductions reach 49.62%, 40.08%, and 56.52% when state retention and ramp-in are jointly applied. These results demonstrate that the proposed framework provides a practical and effective way to improve the robustness and recovery smoothness of real-time PPP under short SSR interruptions.

CO2 Fracturing in Pre-conditioned Reservoir Rocks Maximizes Geothermal Extraction Potential While Minimizing Induced Seismicity

Authors: Lie Kong, Bing Q. Li, Fanlin Ling, W. G. P. Kumari, P. G. Ranjith, Junlong Shang

Institution: University of Manchester, Manchester School of Architecture, Western University, University of Wollongong, Monash University

Published: 2026-07-20

Journal: Rock Mechanics and Rock Engineering

Method: Numerical Simulation

Citations: 0

Abstract:

Abstract Geothermal energy offers a reliable low-carbon pathway for decarbonization by providing continuous heat and baseload power. Enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) further expand this potential by enabling energy recovery from deep, otherwise inaccessible low-permeability crystalline rocks. However, wider deployment remains constrained by challenges in achieving effective reservoir stimulation while minimizing induced seismicity. These limitations largely stem from an incomplete understanding of fluid-driven fracturing mechanisms and fluid–rock interactions in crystalline reservoir rocks. Recently, CO 2 -based fracturing fluids and rock pre-conditioning techniques, which introduce distributed pre-existing damage into the rock mass, have emerged as promising stimulation strategies. Here, we numerically investigate fluid-driven fracturing of granite, a representative EGS reservoir rock, using a coupled hydro-grain-based model (Hydro-GBM) that explicitly captures grain-scale heterogeneity. Different stimulation scenarios were systematically examined by combining fracturing fluids (high-viscosity water versus low-viscosity CO 2 ) with contrasting rock conditions (intact versus pre-conditioned). The underlying grain-scale mechanisms governing fracture development and induced micro-seismicity were also analyzed. The results show that low-viscosity CO 2 reduces breakdown pressure and promotes more distributed fracture development, while rock pre-conditioning accelerates crack initiation, increases fracture complexity, and lowers the intensity of induced seismic responses. Among all scenarios, the hybrid strategy combining CO 2 injection with rock pre-conditioning produced the most favorable outcome, generating the most extensive fracture network while yielding the lowest peak micro-seismic magnitude. Compared with conventional water fracturing in intact rock, this approach increased crack counts by 69% and reduced peak micro-seismic energy by approximately 25%. The enhanced fracture development primarily results from the activation of grain-boundary cracking and pre-existing damage, driven mainly by stress redistribution during fracture propagation and fluid leak-off, with elevated matrix pore pressure playing a secondary role. These findings provide mechanistic insights for designing safer and more effective EGS stimulation strategies. More broadly, the proposed hybrid approach offers a pathway toward improved geothermal energy recovery while reducing water demand, mitigating seismic risk, and enabling potential subsurface carbon storage.
